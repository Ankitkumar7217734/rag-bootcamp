Chapter 10: AI Agents vs Agentic AI

One assistant that answers a question, versus a team of them that plans, delegates, builds, checks each other's work, and learns — and why that difference reshapes how we build with LLMs.

Overview

So far this course has built retrieval pipelines — systems that fetch the right context and hand it to an LLM. But the moment you let the LLM decide what to do next — call a tool, search a database, ask a follow-up, hand work to another model — you've stepped from a pipeline into the world of agents.

Two terms get used almost interchangeably here, and the confusion is costly: "AI agent" and "Agentic AI." They are not the same thing. An AI agent is a single program that performs a specific task with a degree of autonomy. Agentic AI is a broader framework where multiple agents collaborate and make decisions independently to achieve a larger goal — emphasising adaptability and learning over fixed rules.

This chapter draws the line clearly, then makes it concrete with real systems: customer-service chatbots and banking bots on the agent side; smart homes, health assistants, and a full software-development team on the agentic side.

The Core Distinction

An AI agent is a single entity with a focused purpose — it handles a specific task within predefined rules. Agentic AI is a network of agents working together — it focuses on the overall system, with the ability to learn, adapt, and make complex decisions toward a larger goal. Put simply: an AI agent is a worker; agentic AI is the whole self-organising team.

What Is an AI Agent?

An AI agent is an individual software program designed to perform a specific task with a degree of autonomy — that is, without constant human intervention. It perceives an input, applies its logic, and produces an output, all on its own. But its scope is narrow and its behaviour is largely rule-bound.

Two Defining Traits

TraitWhat it means for an AI agent
1 · FunctionalityHandles specific tasks with limited autonomy. It does one job well and stays inside that lane.
2 · Decision MakingDriven by predefined rules. Given the same input, it follows the same fixed logic — it does not improvise a new strategy.

The mental model is a single entity with a focused purpose. There is one agent, it has one job, and the boundaries of that job are drawn in advance.

Example — A Customer-Service Chatbot

The canonical AI agent is a customer-service chatbot. A user asks a question, the bot follows a fixed retrieve-and-answer flow, and it returns a basic answer — "Where is my order?" leads to an order-status lookup and a reply. It is autonomous in that no human types the response, but it never steps outside its script.

What Is Agentic AI?

Agentic AI describes a broader framework in which multiple AI agents collaborate and make decisions independently to achieve a larger goal — emphasising adaptability and learning from experience rather than just following predefined rules. It still solves tasks without human intervention, but it does so by reasoning about the whole system.

Where an AI agent follows a script, agentic AI makes autonomous decisions based on real-time data and circumstances. It focuses on the overall objective and has the ability to learn, adapt, and make complex decisions. The defining structure is a network of agents working together to achieve a goal.

Example — A Smart-Home System

A smart-home system is agentic AI in miniature. Multiple AI subsystems — controlling lights, the thermostat, and appliances — perceive their surroundings, coordinate with each other, and adapt their behaviour toward a shared goal such as reducing electricity use. No single rule dictates the outcome; the agents negotiate it together as conditions change.

The two live on a spectrum of autonomy. The clearest way to keep them straight is feature by feature:

A Useful Rule of Thumb

If the system's behaviour can be fully written down as "if this, then that" ahead of time, you have an AI agent. If the system must decide its own steps, coordinate several specialists, and improve as it sees more data, you have Agentic AI.

The Four Pillars of an Agentic System

What lets agentic AI go beyond a fixed script is a continuous loop of four capabilities. Each pillar feeds the next, and the cycle repeats as the environment changes:

PillarWhat it does
1 · PerceptionGather the data from the surroundings — read the inputs, sensors, documents, or state of the world.
2 · ReasoningUnderstand what is going on — interpret the perceived data and decide what matters.
3 · ActionTake a specific action — call a tool, send a message, change something in the environment.
4 · LearningImprove and adapt over time — update memory and strategy based on the results of past actions.

In pseudocode, the whole of agentic behaviour is this loop running until the goal is met:

while not goal_reached:
    observation = perceive(environment)        # 1 · Perception
    plan        = reason(observation, memory)   # 2 · Reasoning
    result      = act(plan, tools)              # 3 · Action
    memory      = learn(memory, result)         # 4 · Learning

An AI agent usually collapses this to a single pass — perceive the query, apply a rule, act, done. Agentic AI keeps the loop spinning, with multiple agents each running their own version and sharing what they learn.

The Brain of an Agent — LLMs, Tools & Memory

Both AI agents and agentic AI are built from the same raw materials. The LLM is the brain of the agent — it does the reasoning. What turns a plain language model into something that can act is giving that brain two things: tools to affect the world and databases / memory to remember and look things up.

Building blockRoleExample
LLMThe brain — perceives, reasons, and decides what to do nextGPT-4, Claude, Llama
ToolsHands — let the agent take actions beyond textWeb search, calculator, code runner, API calls
Databases / MemoryLong-term knowledge and recall — including the retriever + vector store from earlier chaptersVector DB, SQL, conversation memory
Where This Course Fits In

Everything you built in Chapters 1–9 — chunking, embeddings, vector stores, hybrid search, query enhancement, multimodal retrieval — is the memory an agent reaches into. RAG is how an agent knows things; tools are how it does things; the LLM is how it decides. Agents are where retrieval stops being a pipeline and becomes a capability.

Where Each One Fits — More Examples

The right choice is about the shape of the problem. When the process is clearly defined, an AI agent is simpler, cheaper, and more predictable. When the problem demands flexibility, real-time adaptation, and several specialists, agentic AI earns its complexity.

Use caseTypeWhy
Customer-service chatbotAI AgentAnswers basic questions along a fixed path
Automated banking botAI AgentBalance inquiries and transactions — processes are clearly defined
Smart-home systemAgentic AIMultiple agents coordinate and adapt toward a shared goal
Personalised health assistantAgentic AIAnalyses medical history, real-time health data, and lifestyle factors to give personalised care; adapts to new research and patient feedback

The banking bot and the health assistant make the contrast vivid. The banking bot works because "check balance" and "transfer funds" are clearly defined processes — predefined rules are a feature, not a limitation. The health assistant must weigh history against real-time signals and lifestyle, and keep up with new medical research — its flexibility and complexity are exactly what make it agentic.

Worked Example: An Agentic AI Software-Development Team

The most complete illustration is an agentic system modelled on a real software project for a client (say, XYZ Company). A human software org has three broad stages — requirement gathering (business analyst, product manager), sprint planning (the agile process), and developer teams. An agentic AI mirrors that structure with collaborating agents.

Requirements flow into an LLM planner that decomposes them into tasks. Each task goes to a developer agent — itself an AI agent powered by an LLM and careful prompt engineering. Their output is checked by a testing agent and a code-review agent; failures raise bugs that loop straight back to the developers. A human stays in the loop throughout, steering and approving — until the project ships.

How the Pieces Map

StageHuman teamAgentic AI equivalent
Requirement gatheringBusiness analyst, product managerRequirements captured and fed to the LLM planner
Sprint planningAgile processLLM planner decomposes work into Task 1, 2, 3
DevelopmentDeveloper teamsDev agents (LLM + prompt engineering), one per task

OversightTech lead / stakeholdersHuman feedback in the loop, steering every stage
Why This Is Agentic, Not Just Many Agents

Each developer agent on its own is a plain AI agent. What makes the system agentic is the coordination: a planner that decides how to split the work, agents that hand off to testing and review, a bug loop that sends failures back for another pass, and a human steering the whole thing. The intelligence lives in the network, not in any single box.

Another Example: A Blog-Generation Agentic System

The same pattern generalises far beyond code. A blog-generation agentic system chains specialist agents into a content pipeline, each doing one job and passing its output to the next — with the planner (and a human) keeping the whole thing on track:

AgentResponsibility
Research agentGathers sources and facts on the topic (often via RAG)
Outline agentTurns the research into a structured outline
Writing agentDrafts each section from the outline
Editing agentRevises for clarity, tone, and accuracy
SEO agentOptimises titles, headings, and keywords before publishing

Swap the agents and you have a research assistant, a data-analysis crew, or a support-triage system. The framework — plan, delegate, act, review, learn — stays the same.

Key Takeaways

Remember These Five

1. An AI agent is a single program that performs a specific task with limited autonomy, driven by predefined rules — a single entity with a focused purpose (e.g. a customer-service chatbot or banking bot).

2. Agentic AI is a broader framework where multiple agents collaborate and make autonomous decisions to reach a larger goal, learning and adapting rather than just following rules (e.g. a smart home or health assistant).

3. Every agentic system runs the same four-pillar loop — Perception → Reasoning → Action → Learning — over and over as the environment changes.

4. The LLM is the brain; tools are its hands; databases / memory (your RAG stack) are its knowledge. Agents are where retrieval becomes a capability instead of a pipeline.

5. Choose by problem shape: clearly-defined process → AI agent; complex, adaptive, multi-specialist workflow → Agentic AI. The software-dev team — planner, dev agents, testing & review, a bug loop, and a human in the loop — shows agentic AI end to end.
